# Core Framework

Use these concepts as modern decision tools. Do not treat them as fixed slogans.

## Investigation and Research

Start from facts. Separate:

- facts: observed or verifiable conditions
- assumptions: plausible but unverified claims
- judgments: interpretations of facts
- unknowns: missing variables that could change the answer

Useful prompts:

- What is the real objective?
- What facts are known?
- Which claims are secondhand or emotional?
- What facts would change the decision?
- What is the cheapest way to learn those facts?

## Concrete Analysis

Do not mechanically copy past examples. Check time, place, rules, resources, opponent, stage, incentives, and available feedback.

Use this when the user copies a strategy from another company, team, player, market, or historical case. Identify which conditions transfer and which do not.

## Contradiction Analysis

Find the principal contradiction: the problem whose movement most shapes the whole situation. Then find the principal aspect: the side currently driving the contradiction.

Modern examples:

- Startup: not "we lack resources" but "we have not found a narrow segment where our speed beats incumbent scale."
- Team: not "people disagree" but "the team lacks shared evidence for choosing between two product bets."
- Esports: not "we are behind" but "we cannot contest vision safely before the next objective."

## Global and Local

Judge local actions by whether they serve the global objective. A local win can be wasteful if it does not change the larger position. A local retreat can be correct if it preserves resources for a decisive point.

Ask:

- What is the global win condition?
- Which local point has decisive influence?
- Which local fights are distractions?
- What can be conceded without changing the overall outcome?

## Stage Analysis

Strategy depends on stage. Early, middle, and late stages have different tasks.

Common stage errors:

- using late-stage scaling tactics before product-market evidence
- seeking decisive confrontation before local advantage exists
- continuing early exploration after conditions require focused execution
- over-investing in polish when the principal contradiction is demand or distribution

## Strategic Endurance and Tactical Resolution

Keep patience for long-term objectives, but make local actions concrete and time-bounded.

Use this distinction:

- strategic endurance: sustain direction under long-cycle uncertainty
- tactical resolution: create fast local evidence, wins, or corrections

Avoid two errors:

- calling delay "long-termism"
- expecting whole-system victory from one local success

## Concentration of Advantage

Limited resources must not be spread evenly across all fronts. Choose where to create local advantage in time, attention, money, skill, information, distribution, or coordination.

Ask:

- What should be paused or downgraded?
- What single point would unlock the next stage?
- Where can current resources create disproportionate effect?
- What is the exit condition if the concentrated bet fails?

## Initiative

Initiative means creating options under constraint. It is not blind aggression.

Ways to create initiative:

- run a reversible test
- force hidden information into the open
- choose the timing of a decision
- change the comparison set
- move to a narrower field where current strengths matter
- create a resource trade the opponent or problem must answer

## Practice Feedback

Treat plans as hypotheses. Return to actual users, teammates, customers, match replays, market data, or field evidence.

For each recommendation, define:

- what to test
- how long to test
- what success means
- what failure means
- what evidence triggers revision

## Stakeholder Alignment

Translate united-front thinking into lawful, non-coercive stakeholder analysis.

Classify:

- core supporters
- potential allies
- neutral parties
- blockers
- decision makers
- affected but unheard groups

Aim to expand legitimate cooperation and reduce unnecessary conflict. Do not use this framework to manipulate or target real people.
